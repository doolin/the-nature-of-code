Energy states in the hydrogen atom

The hydrogen atom, with its single proton and single electron, admits a solution to the governing Schrödinger equation, wave functions which probabilistically describe the electron's position. Schrödinger's equation is defined by

$$ \left( -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \nabla^2 + V \right) \psi = i \hbar \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial t}, $$

where $\hbar$ is the reduced Planck constant, $m$ is the mass of the electron, $V$ is the potential energy, and $\psi$ is the wave function. The wave function is a complex-valued function of position and time, and the probability of finding the electron at a given position is the square of the absolute value of the wave function. The wave function is normalized, so that the integral of the square of the absolute value of the wave function over all space is 1. [GPT-4o 2024-08-01]
