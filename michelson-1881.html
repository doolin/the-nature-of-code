Michelson's 1881 Ether Drift Experiment

In 1881, Albert A. Michelson conducted an experiment to detect the motion of Earth through the hypothetical "luminiferous ether" - the medium through which light was thought to propagate. The experiment used an interferometer to measure the difference in travel time for light beams traveling parallel and perpendicular to Earth's motion through the ether.

The experiment famously produced a "null result" - no detectable difference in the light travel times, which was unexpected if the ether existed. This null result was one of the key pieces of evidence that eventually led to the development of Einstein's theory of special relativity.

The Michelson Interferometer

The Michelson interferometer splits a beam of light into two perpendicular paths using a half-silvered mirror (beam splitter). One beam travels parallel to the direction of Earth's motion through the ether, while the other travels perpendicular to it. After reflecting off mirrors, both beams recombine and create an interference pattern.

If Earth were moving through the ether at velocity \(v\), the time for light to travel the parallel path would be: (CHECK THESE EQUATIONS FOR CORRCTNESS, THEY ARE CURSOR-GENERATED)

$$t_{\parallel} = \frac{2L}{c} \frac{1}{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}$$

And the time for the perpendicular path would be:

$$t_{\perp} = \frac{2L}{c} \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}$$

The difference in these times would cause a shift in the interference pattern that should be detectable. However, Michelson observed no such shift.

The Null Result

Michelson's experiment was designed to detect Earth's orbital velocity around the Sun, which is approximately 30 km/s. The expected fringe shift was about 0.04 wavelengths, which should have been easily detectable with the precision of the interferometer.

The null result was initially puzzling and led to several theories to explain it, including:

  • Ether drag hypothesis: The ether might be dragged along with Earth
  • Lorentz contraction: Objects might contract in the direction of motion
  • Time dilation: Time might slow down for moving objects

These ideas eventually coalesced into Einstein's special theory of relativity, which eliminated the need for an ether entirely.

Historical Significance

Michelson's 1881 experiment, along with the more precise 1887 Michelson-Morley experiment, played a crucial role in the development of modern physics. The null result was one of the key pieces of evidence that led to the abandonment of the ether theory and the acceptance of Einstein's special relativity.

The experiment also demonstrated the power of interferometry as a precision measurement technique, which continues to be used in modern physics experiments, including the detection of gravitational waves by LIGO.
